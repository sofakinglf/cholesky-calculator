Topic #09

Cholesky Decomposition

Discussion Examples Calculator
01

Mathematical Discussion

Cholesky decomposition (pronounced shuh-LES-kee) is a factorization of a symmetric positive-definite (SPD) matrix $A$ into the product of a lower-triangular matrix $L$ and its transpose:

$$A = L L^T$$

Named after French military officer André-Louis Cholesky (1875–1918), it is roughly twice as efficient as LU decomposition when the matrix is known to be SPD, since it exploits the symmetry to halve the work.

Required Conditions

C1

Square: $A$ must be $n \times n$.

C2

Symmetric: $A = A^T$, i.e., $a_{ij} = a_{ji}$ for all $i, j$.

C3

Positive-definite: $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} > 0$ for all non-zero $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n$. Equivalently, all leading principal minors (and hence all eigenvalues) are positive.

—

Derivation of the Formulas

Expanding $A = LL^T$ entry by entry, each entry $a_{ij}$ satisfies:

$$a_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^{\min(i,j)} l_{ik}\, l_{jk}$$

Solving column by column (left to right), for column $j$ of $L$:

Diagonal entry ($i = j$):

$$l_{jj} = \sqrt{a_{jj} - \sum_{k=1}^{j-1} l_{jk}^2}$$

Sub-diagonal entry ($i > j$):

$$l_{ij} = \frac{1}{l_{jj}}\!\left(a_{ij} - \sum_{k=1}^{j-1} l_{ik}\,l_{jk}\right)$$

The upper triangle of $L$ is zero (it is strictly lower-triangular plus diagonal).

Complexity

$O(n^3 / 3)$ operations — about half that of Gaussian elimination on a general matrix.

Uniqueness

The decomposition is unique when $A$ is SPD and all diagonal entries of $L$ are taken positive.

Numerical Stability

Cholesky is unconditionally stable for SPD matrices—no pivoting is required.

Applications

Solving $Ax = b$, Monte Carlo simulation (Gaussian sampling), least-squares, and finite-element methods.

—

Solving a Linear System $Ax = b$

Once $A = LL^T$ is known, solve in two triangular sweeps:

Algorithm

01

Forward substitution — solve $L\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{b}$ for $\mathbf{y}$: $$y_i = \frac{1}{l_{ii}}\!\left(b_i - \sum_{k=1}^{i-1} l_{ik}\,y_k\right)$$

02

Backward substitution — solve $L^T\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{y}$ for $\mathbf{x}$: $$x_i = \frac{1}{l_{ii}}\!\left(y_i - \sum_{k=i+1}^{n} l_{ki}\,x_k\right)$$

02

Worked Examples

1

Cholesky Decomposition of a 3×3 Matrix

Decompose $A$ and verify $A = LL^T$

Given

Symmetric positive-definite matrix:

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 & -2 \\ 2 & 10 & 2 \\ -2 & 2 & 5 \end{bmatrix}$$
Step 1

Column $j=1$: compute $l_{11}$

$$l_{11} = \sqrt{a_{11}} = \sqrt{4} = 2$$

Sub-diagonal entries of column 1:

$$l_{21} = \frac{a_{21}}{l_{11}} = \frac{2}{2} = 1$$ $$l_{31} = \frac{a_{31}}{l_{11}} = \frac{-2}{2} = -1$$
Step 2

Column $j=2$: compute $l_{22}$

$$l_{22} = \sqrt{a_{22} - l_{21}^2} = \sqrt{10 - 1^2} = \sqrt{9} = 3$$

Sub-diagonal entry of column 2:

$$l_{32} = \frac{a_{32} - l_{31}l_{21}}{l_{22}} = \frac{2 - (-1)(1)}{3} = \frac{3}{3} = 1$$
Step 3

Column $j=3$: compute $l_{33}$

$$l_{33} = \sqrt{a_{33} - l_{31}^2 - l_{32}^2} = \sqrt{5 - (-1)^2 - 1^2} = \sqrt{3} \approx 1.7321$$
Result
$$L = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 3 & 0 \\ -1 & 1 & \sqrt{3} \end{bmatrix}$$

Verification — $LL^T$:

$$LL^T = \begin{bmatrix}2&0&0\\1&3&0\\-1&1&\sqrt3\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}2&1&-1\\0&3&1\\0&0&\sqrt3\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}4&2&-2\\2&10&2\\-2&2&5\end{bmatrix} = A \checkmark$$
2

Solving $Ax = b$ Using Cholesky

Forward and backward substitution after factorisation

Given
$$A = \begin{bmatrix}25 & 15 & -5 \\ 15 & 18 & 0 \\ -5 & 0 & 11\end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix}35 \\ 33 \\ 6\end{bmatrix}$$
Step 1

Cholesky Factorisation

$$l_{11} = \sqrt{25} = 5$$ $$l_{21} = \frac{15}{5} = 3, \quad l_{31} = \frac{-5}{5} = -1$$ $$l_{22} = \sqrt{18 - 3^2} = \sqrt{9} = 3$$ $$l_{32} = \frac{0 - (-1)(3)}{3} = \frac{3}{3} = 1$$ $$l_{33} = \sqrt{11 - (-1)^2 - 1^2} = \sqrt{9} = 3$$
$$L = \begin{bmatrix}5 & 0 & 0 \\ 3 & 3 & 0 \\ -1 & 1 & 3\end{bmatrix}$$
Step 2

Forward substitution — solve $L\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{b}$

$$y_1 = \frac{35}{5} = 7$$ $$y_2 = \frac{33 - 3(7)}{3} = \frac{33 - 21}{3} = \frac{12}{3} = 4$$ $$y_3 = \frac{6 - (-1)(7) - (1)(4)}{3} = \frac{6 + 7 - 4}{3} = \frac{9}{3} = 3$$

$\mathbf{y} = [7,\ 4,\ 3]^T$

Step 3

Backward substitution — solve $L^T\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{y}$

$$x_3 = \frac{3}{3} = 1$$ $$x_2 = \frac{4 - (1)(1)}{3} = \frac{3}{3} = 1$$ $$x_1 = \frac{7 - (3)(1) - (-1)(1)}{5} = \frac{7 - 3 + 1}{5} = \frac{5}{5} = 1$$
Solution
$$\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix}1\\1\\1\end{bmatrix}$$

Verification — $A\mathbf{x}$: $$25(1)+15(1)-5(1)=35,\quad 15(1)+18(1)+0(1)=33,\quad -5(1)+0(1)+11(1)=6 \checkmark$$
